BELL: SIX BUCKS FOR THE MAYOR’S EAR — AND NENSHI STAYS QUIET

A $6 podcast subscription buys Discord access and lunch with Calgary Mayor Jeromy Farkas. So why is Alberta NDP Leader Naheed Nenshi saying nothing?

Author of the article:
Rick Bell
Published Apr 13, 2026 • Last updated 2 days ago • 4 minute read
Join the conversation

You can save this article by registering for free here. Or sign-in if you have an account.

Article content

Only in Calgary.

That’s the line people use when something doesn’t quite pass the smell test but nobody in the political class seems eager to poke at it too hard.

So let’s walk through this slowly.

For six dollars a month — about what you’d spend on a coffee and a muffin — you can subscribe to a podcast that, not that long ago, had a Liberal in the ownership mix. That would be Corey Hogan, now in Ottawa and no longer tied to the remaining hosts.

Fair enough. People move on.

But the six bucks doesn’t just get you audio.

It gets you in the room.

A Discord server, private, curated, where the conversation flows a little more freely and the barriers between politics and the public aren’t quite so high.

And right there in that space?

The sitting mayor of Calgary.

Jeromy Farkas.

Not as a one-off guest.

Not as a drop-in for a quick hello.

But as someone meeting subscribers, organizing lunches, and spending time with people who, however you slice it, paid to be part of that circle.

Now, maybe that’s just the evolution of politics.

Maybe town halls are out and Discord is in.

Maybe this is just what engagement looks like now.

But it also raises a question that doesn’t go away just because the price tag is small.

When access to a sitting mayor runs through a paid subscription, even a modest one, what exactly is that?

Grassroots connection?

Or something that starts to look a little too much like pay-to-play access, whether anyone wants to use that phrase or not?

And that’s where this gets interesting.

Because sitting just up the political ladder is Naheed Nenshi.

The leader of the Alberta NDP.

A guy who built his brand on accountability, on calling things out, on making noise when the lines between politics and influence start to blur.

And on this?

Nothing.

No comment.

No concern.

No curiosity about whether this is the kind of access that should raise eyebrows.

And it’s hard not to notice, because if you flip the situation — change the players, change the platform, change the political branding — does anyone seriously believe the silence would hold?

Or would we be hearing about it, loudly, repeatedly, with questions about fairness, optics, and who exactly gets a seat at the table?

That’s the part that sticks.

Not the six dollars.

Not the podcast.

But the quiet.

Because in politics, silence is rarely an accident.

And when access starts to look like something you can subscribe to, even for pocket change, people are going to wonder who’s watching the store.

And who decided this one wasn’t worth talking about.
